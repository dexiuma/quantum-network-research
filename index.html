An integrated framework for
quantum network research

This framework integrates three components: a systematic literature survey of quantum networks, QuantumPaperMiner for the collection and generation of quantum data and equations, and a quantum network simulator for modelling network nodes and links spanning superconducting hardware and photonic channels.

Component 03 · Collection + generation

Quantum network simulator

A two-node-and-beyond quantum network built on Qiskit, with custom error models for long-distance communication. Nodes are calibrated from real superconducting hardware; links model photonic fiber / wireless channels.

N

Nodes — superconducting QPUs

Six interchangeable node-error modes: pure local Aer, device-averaged digital twin (fitted from real T1/T2 & gate-error history), layout-aware digital twin (only the qubits a job actually runs on), Qiskit Aer's own device model, equations (rates derived from device physics), and live IBM hardware submission.

L

Links — photonic channels

Each link carries its own distance-dependent channel error model for fiber or free-space transmission.

E

Custom error models

Seven physical error types: fiber attenuation, polarization drift, phase noise, timing jitter, dark counts, environmental decoherence, chromatic dispersion.

P

Protocols

Entanglement distribution, quantum teleportation, BB84 QKD, and distance-sweep fidelity analysis.

Error models

Error types by layer

Errors are organised into two layers. Node-level errors act on local operations at each endpoint; channel-level errors act during transmission and scale with distance. Every error is implemented as a Qiskit quantum channel.

This node/link split is the app-wide taxonomy: every chart, comparison, and report categorises errors the same way. All types are built on five primitive channels — pauli, depolarizing, amplitude_damping, phase_damping, thermal_relaxation — and users can define additional custom errors at either layer (splice loss, WDM crosstalk, repeater noise, an eavesdropper model, …) with their own probability and distance scaling (linear, exponential, constant, per-splice, or per-repeater), from the UI or a config file.

Fiber attenuation — the Beer–Lambert law
$$T=10^{-\alpha L/10},\qquad p_\text{det}=T\,\eta_d$$
Standard fiber-optics attenuation: transmission falls tenfold for every 10 dB of loss — at α = 0.2 dB/km, 10% of photons survive 50 km and 1% survive 100 km.
Symbols: T — fraction of photons that survive the fiber · p_det — probability a sent photon is actually detected (survival × detector efficiency).
Source: Corning SMF-28 optical fiber specification; N. Gisin, G. Ribordy, W. Tittel & H. Zbinden, "Quantum cryptography", Rev. Mod. Phys. 74, 145 (2002).
Chromatic dispersion — pulse broadening
$$\Delta\tau=D\,\Delta\lambda\,L$$
Different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds, spreading the photon’s wave packet in time as it propagates.
Symbols: Δτ — pulse broadening (ps) · D = 17 ps/(nm·km) — dispersion coefficient of SMF-28 fiber at 1550 nm · Δλ = 0.1 nm — spectral linewidth of the photon source.
Source: G. P. Agrawal, Fiber-Optic Communication Systems, 4th ed. (Wiley, 2010), ch. 2.
Damping channels — amplitude & phase
$$K_0^{\text{AD}}=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\[2pt]0&\sqrt{1-\gamma}\end{pmatrix},\ K_1^{\text{AD}}=\begin{pmatrix}0&\sqrt{\gamma}\\[2pt]0&0\end{pmatrix}$$ $$K_0^{\text{PD}}=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\[2pt]0&\sqrt{1-\lambda}\end{pmatrix},\ K_1^{\text{PD}}=\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\[2pt]0&\sqrt{\lambda}\end{pmatrix}$$
The two standard damping channels: amplitude damping (AD) models energy loss toward |0⟩ and is used for attenuation effects; phase damping (PD) models loss of phase information without changing the bit value and is used for phase noise.
Symbols: γ — probability an arrived |1⟩ decays toward |0⟩ · λ — probability the qubit’s phase information is lost.
Source: M. A. Nielsen & I. L. Chuang, Quantum Computation and Quantum Information (Cambridge, 2010), §8.3.5–8.3.6.
Pauli channels & thermal relaxation
$$\mathcal{E}(\rho)=\Big(1-\textstyle\sum_P p_P\Big)\,\rho+\!\!\sum_{P\in\{X,Y,Z\}}\!\! p_P\,P\rho P$$ $$T_2\le 2T_1$$
The Pauli-channel family (bit flip, phase flip, depolarizing) used for polarization drift, timing jitter, and dark counts; and thermal T₁/T₂ relaxation used for environmental decoherence, with the physical constraint T₂ ≤ 2T₁ enforced.
Symbols: p_P — probability of Pauli error P · T₁ — energy-relaxation time (how long |1⟩ survives) · T₂ — dephasing time (how long phase survives).
Source: Nielsen & Chuang, §8.3.3–8.3.4; T₂ ≤ 2T₁: P. Krantz et al., "A quantum engineer’s guide to superconducting qubits", Appl. Phys. Rev. 6, 021318 (2019).
Composition — one channel per hop
$$\mathcal{E}_\text{link}=\mathcal{E}_n\circ\cdots\circ\mathcal{E}_1$$ $$\mathcal{E}_\text{path}=\mathcal{E}_\text{hop}^{(N-1)}\circ\cdots\circ\mathcal{E}_\text{hop}^{(1)}$$
The link’s enabled error channels are composed in sequence into one CPTP transmission error per link, bound to the id gate (the simulator’s convention for "in transit"). Multi-hop paths compose one such error per hop.
Symbols: ℰ₁…ℰₙ — the link’s enabled error channels, applied in order (rightmost first) · ℰ_link — the whole link’s combined channel · ℰ_hop⁽ⁱ⁾ — the combined channel of the i-th link on a routed path · N — number of nodes on the path (N−1 hops) · CPTP — "completely positive, trace-preserving": a mathematically valid quantum channel.
Source: channel composition is standard CPTP calculus — Nielsen & Chuang, §8.2.4.

Assumptions & scope. The relations above are the textbook-grounded pieces of the link model, shown with their sources. Connecting them into per-channel error probabilities — how much effective damping a given loss level causes on received qubits, how fast polarization drifts per kilometre, how temperature and detector jitter map to dephasing strengths — is done by monotonic, saturating strength mappings implemented in the simulator (QuantumNetworkErrorModel). Those coupling coefficients are phenomenological engineering choices rather than published results, so they are deliberately not displayed as physics here; replacing them with literature-sourced values is planned work for the QuantumPaperMiner pipeline. Two further standing assumptions: photon loss is heralded in a real network but is modeled as effective damping on the qubits that do arrive (with the 90% detector efficiency this gives every link a ~10% damping floor even at 0 km), and the reported Bell fidelity is the Z-basis correlation P(00)+P(11) — a phase-insensitive upper bound on true state fidelity. At the default 50 km, Beer–Lambert gives T = 0.100 and a detection probability of 0.090.

Use 1 · IBM-calibrated

Digital-twin node

  • Averages the full collected calibration history (378 snapshots and counting) into a stable fitted noise model (mean, std, min/max per parameter); a layout-aware variant instead averages only the qubits a job actually runs on.
  • Reports a fit-quality score of 1 − CV (coefficient of variation) — lower device drift means a more reliable twin.
  • Builds a local Aer NoiseModel: depolarizing on 1- and 2-qubit gates, thermal relaxation (T₁/T₂) on idle, and an empirically fitted asymmetric readout matrix — real devices misread |1⟩ more often than |0⟩, and the twin reproduces that.
  • Runs entirely offline with no quota — a faithful stand-in for the real QPU inside larger network simulations.
Use 2 · IBM-live

Real-time live node

  • Transpiles the circuit for the target backend and submits it via Qiskit Runtime SamplerV2.
  • Jobs run asynchronously — the node returns a job ID that is polled until real measurement counts return.
  • Every submission is quota-guarded by a usage tracker against the 10 min/month budget: it estimates, warns, and blocks before spending time — then records the actually billed seconds after each job.
  • Falls back gracefully: live → stored snapshot → typical defaults, so simulations never stall when hardware is unreachable.
Results

Experiment — a three-node relay, run four ways

The validation experiment (July 2026): a 447-character paragraph travels from node A through 50 km of simulated fiber to a repeater node B, and on through another 50 km to node C — the trusted-relay layout real long-haul quantum networks use. The same message, same pipeline, and numerically identical fiber stages are run four times, changing only one thing: where the node (quantum-processor) errors come from. Three of the four runs never touch a quantum computer — the point is to measure how well each free model predicts the real machine.

Mode 1 · Ground truth

Real IBM hardware

Every node stage executes on the real ibm_marrakesh superconducting processor: three batched jobs (encode at A, repeat at B, receive at C), 27 s of billed quantum time each. This run is the answer the other three try to predict.

Mode 2 · Simulated

Digital twin — device-averaged

A local noise model built from the machine's public calibration data, averaged over all 156 qubits of the device and over months of collected snapshots. It answers: "how noisy is this machine, typically?" Runs offline, costs nothing.

Mode 3 · Simulated

Digital twin — layout-aware

The same idea, sharpened: calibration averaged over only the ten qubits this job actually runs on (recovered from the submitted circuits), including each qubit's direction-dependent read-out error. It answers: "how noisy will this specific job be?"

Mode 4 · Simulated

Equations model

A closed-form analytical model: gate, decoherence, and read-out error rates derived from device physics (T₁/T₂ coherence times, gate durations, temperature) using no measured data at all — the kind of model you could write down from a textbook.

In the simulated modes the error model is fully customizable: each of the simulator's 11 built-in error channels — 7 fiber-link channels (attenuation, which absorbs photon loss and detector inefficiency; polarization drift; phase noise; timing jitter; chromatic dispersion; dark counts; environmental decoherence) and 4 on-chip node channels (1-qubit gate, 2-qubit gate, read-out, thermal relaxation) — can be individually included or excluded per run, and user-defined custom errors can be added on top. The hardware mode, by definition, arrives with the machine's full error budget. A per-channel ablation in the full report shows that in this experiment read-out error alone accounts for over 98% of every node stage's contribution.

Why the three models rank the way they do

Averaging a whole chip vs modelling your qubits

  • The device-averaged twin lands above the real error rates (+0.35 pp): a chip's 156 qubits vary widely, and a whole-device average is dragged up by its mediocre ones — while an actual job touches only ten specific qubits, which here were better than the chip average.
  • The equations model knows nothing about the machine, and its textbook constants happen to sit a little further from those ten qubits' true rates (+0.55 pp). That it is beaten by one twin and beats the other is the interesting part: a measured model is only better than a physics model if it measures the right thing.
  • The layout-aware twin measures exactly the right thing — the job's own ten qubits, with their measured direction-dependent read-out errors — and lands within +0.08 pp of the real machine at every stage.
What the two twins give users

Two questions, two free predictions

  • Device-averaged twin — the stable, drift-averaged picture of a machine as a whole. Use it to compare devices, plan capacity, and design experiments before knowing which qubits a job will be assigned.
  • Layout-aware twin — a pre-flight forecast of a specific job: predict a run's error budget, choose shot counts, or decide whether hardware is worth it — before spending any of the monthly quantum-time quota.
  • Both run entirely offline from freely downloadable calibration data. In this experiment the three simulated runs together cost nothing; the hardware ground truth cost 81 s of quantum time.

Full methodology, calibration tables, provenance, and the per-channel error breakdown: download the experiment report (PDF). Raw per-shot records of all four runs are persisted in the simulator's data store.
